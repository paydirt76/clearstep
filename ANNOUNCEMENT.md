One turn, one prompt, one answer — wordsmith the question, get a better response. Agentic AI broke that. The model now runs across many turns, queues up work it'll come back to later, and executes long chains of tasks without you watching every step. The bottleneck shifted from phrasing to attention: <strong>what context does the model need in front of it right now to focus?</strong></p><p>That's <strong>context engineering</strong> — making sure each turn gets the right files, the right line ranges, the right history, and nothing else, so the model concentrates on the work instead of rediscovering its surroundings. Too little context and it flails. Too much and the signal drowns in noise. Either way, the answer drifts and the next task starts from a worse place.</p><p>The default agentic tool punts the context-engineering work onto the model itself. <em>"Figure out what you need, then go get it."</em> That's asking the agent to be omniscient — to know in advance what it doesn't know yet, then reach into a codebase it hasn't read and pull the right files. Hand a new coworker a project and say "figure out what you need to know, then start working" — you'd come back to a disaster. Hours of flailing, dozens of files opened, the wrong assumptions baked into the wrong artifacts. The opposite default punts the work onto you: manually attaching files to every prompt, watching the context window fill up, deciding what to evict. That's not engineering — that's babysitting.</p><p>Clear Step puts the context-engineering work in the <strong>plan file</strong>. Each step pre-declares the files and line ranges it needs. When <code>/step</code> runs, it loads exactly those under a 50k-token budget, executes against a focused window, then sharpens the next step's Context with what it just learned. The agent assembles its own context — from a structure you wrote once that improves as work progresses. You don't manually pick which files the AI sees on every turn. You don't ask the model to be omniscient. You write the plan once, and the plan teaches itself.</p><p>That's the unlock: agentic leverage without either failure mode. Not asking the model to be omniscient. Not asking yourself to be a full-time context curator. The plan file is the third option, and it compounds across sessions.</p><hr><h2>How It Works</h2><p>Checkpoints only work when the tool makes them frictionless. Clear Step keeps the plan <strong>on disk, not in the conversation.</strong></p><p>Four architectural moves make step N+1 happen without you reloading context in your head:</p><ol><li><strong>A unique <code>[n]</code> marker</strong> points at the next action across the whole plan. <code>grep</code> finds it, read 60 lines around it, execute. No full-plan reload.</li><li><strong>Context hints per step</strong> — each step pre-declares the files and line ranges it needs. No flailing rediscovery.</li><li><strong>Phased reads</strong> under a 50k-token budget. Phase A orients, Phase B reads history, Phase C reads the current step + its Context files. No double-loading.</li><li><strong>Write-through updates.</strong> After the step runs, the tool writes a timestamped <code>**Results:**</code> block, advances <code>[n]</code> forward, and sharpens the next step's Context with what was just learned.</li></ol><p>The plan becomes a self-sharpening state machine. The conversation is disposable.</p><p>Anyone can ship "one step at a time." The hard part is making step N+1 happen without you reloading context in your head. The product isn't the step discipline — it's the friction removal that makes step discipline sustainable.</p><p>The next section is a little dense. It's where the rubber meets the road. It beeps every few minutes and waits for you to say go. You pay 30 seconds every five minutes so you don't pay 30 minutes of cleanup every hour.</p><p><strong>The main failure mode is different from the tradeoff.</strong></p><p>Clear Step executes the plan you wrote. If Step 2 was built on a misread of the codebase, everything after Step 4 is contaminated. Two recovery paths:</p><ul><li><strong>Cheap.</strong> A mandatory five-question reflection pass after every step (<code>5f</code>) catches roughly 70% of plan drift. Affected downstream steps get marked <code>[~]</code> superseded; corrections get inserted.</li><li><strong>Expensive.</strong> A step gets marked <code>[!]</code> stuck, execution stops, you talk to the model and either rewrite the plan tail or start fresh informed by what was learned.</li></ul><p>The tool bounds execution, not judgment. A bad premise all the way down is still on you.</p><p><strong>What it isn't good for:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Greenfield prototyping.</strong> Use chat mode.
